Analytical Overview

What Was Studied

Whether intermittent vs continuous amikacin antibiotic lock therapy selects for antimicrobial resistance in E. coli biofilms, using in vitro and in vivo catheter models.

What Was Found

Intermittent exposure drove convergent resistance mutations (fusA, sbmA, cpxA) and MIC increases across all tested conditions. Continuous 10-day treatment eradicated biofilms without resistance emergence.

Why This Matters

The authors interpret these findings as evidence that biofilms subjected to intermittent antibiotic lock therapy may accumulate low-level resistance mutations that could otherwise go undetected in routine clinical monitoring.

Biofilm Growth & Inocula

Biofilm establishment: 48-h continuous LB flow (LB experiments); 72-h TPN (TPN & in vivo). 3-day recovery phase after ALT cycles. Inocula: ~10² CFU/100 µL (LF82 & 55989, in vitro LB); 10⁶ CFU/100 µL (55989pAT881, in vitro TPN); 1 × 10⁷ CFU (in vivo). TIVAP volume ≈ 250 µL; flow rate 300 µL/min.

Comparators

ALTPBS served as the primary control. Additional comparisons: ancestor populations (MIC baselines); continuous 10-day vs 7-day ALTAMK; intermittent ALTAMK vs PBS; LB vs TPN context where explicitly tested.

Cross-Condition Synthesis

fusA mutations appeared in all four intermittent ALTAMK conditions at ≥ 5% frequency. Most conditions showed 1.5- to 2.0-fold MIC increases (4 → 6–8 µg/mL MH; 16 → 24–32 µg/mL LB). Rat 6 was exceptional: 6-fold rise (4 → 24 µg/mL MH; 16 → 96 µg/mL LB). In vivo resistance appeared to develop faster and at higher levels than in vitro.

Mechanistic & Causal / Associative Relations

Resistance-Linked Mutations

Loss-of-function mutations in sbmA and mutations in fusA have previously been associated with increased aminoglycoside resistance. Deleting sbmA reduces aminoglycoside susceptibility.

Supported Results; Discussion

Elongation factor G mutations selectively silence drug-bound ribosomes, preventing error cluster formation and reducing the second self-promoted uptake phase of aminoglycosides by preserving proteome and membrane integrity.

Supported Discussion

Mutations in nuoA·C/D and nuoC/D·N genes are linked to aminoglycoside resistance, tolerance, and persistence.

Supported Discussion
Stress Response & Efflux Regulation

Mutations in cpxA can confer aminoglycoside resistance by regulating acrD, encoding the AcrD transporter of the AcrAD-TolC efflux pump. CpxRA senses cell-envelope stresses and regulates downstream genes including acrD.

Supported Results; Discussion

cpxA single amino acid substitutions could alter CpxRA functionality and affect regulons including acrD.

Proposed Discussion
Biofilm Adaptation

Enhanced mutation rate in biofilms increases the likelihood of accumulating emergent resistance mutations even without antibiotic pressure.

Supported Results

fimA/fimH mutations selected by biofilm lifestyle may affect biofilm-forming capacity and tolerance to amikacin, promoting emergence of resistance mutations.

Constraints & Uncertainty

  • Small in vivo sample size (n = 3 per group) may limit statistical power.
  • Tested strains and nutritional conditions may not fully mimic clinical settings.
  • Mechanisms behind rapid and higher-level in vivo resistance remain unclear.
  • In vivo TPN was instilled, not continuously infused, because rats could not be immobilised.
  • Systemic ceftriaxone in vivo may have influenced amikacin resistance dynamics.
  • Authors suggest further work using a continuous drip infusion pig catheter model.

Interpretive Signals from the Authors

Resistance Evolution & Biofilm Dynamics

The authors interpret these findings as evidence that biofilms may act as evolutionary incubators in which medical interventions unintentionally influence adaptation outcomes.

Proposed

Low-level resistance in treated biofilms may be overlooked clinically and may contribute to selection of high-level resistant mutants.

Proposed

High-level resistance is selectively promoted during treatment when nutrient levels are high.

Proposed
Molecular Contributors

fimA/fimH mutations may have increased biofilm formation, persister frequency, and overall tolerance.

Proposed

cpxA mutants are likely linked with amikacin resistance; other mutated genes (MFS transporters, nuo genes) may collectively contribute to low-level resistance.

Proposed
Clinical & Translational Implications

Optimising antimicrobial timing could mitigate resistance emergence. The principle may extend to other biofilm-related infections exposed to short-term dosing.

Proposed

Intermittent treatment may occur in other infections due to poor adherence, adversely affecting efficacy. Further research is needed to optimise dose and schedule for intermittent regimens.

Proposed
