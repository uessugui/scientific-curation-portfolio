Bottom line
Two specific lesions—RamR T116I and a 1 bp insertion upstream of ramA—drive tigecycline resistance in K. pneumoniae through a coherent, experimentally supported mechanism. The evidence is directionally strong and validation-ready, but not yet standardized enough for quantitative claims.
Why this matters

This evidence package is commercially useful because it does more than flag association. It links lesion, regulatory consequence, promoter binding, efflux activity, and resistance phenotype into a coherent decision narrative.

That makes it relevant for target assessment, resistance surveillance strategy, candidate validation planning, and go/no-go prioritization around RamR-pathway biology.

Overall confidence
Evidence strength: Moderate Mechanistic confidence: Moderate Translation/practical: Moderate

Confidence is not higher because the body of evidence is directionally consistent but not standardized well enough for strong magnitude claims or clean single-lesion generalization.

What is supported
  • Introducing RamR T116I can increase tigecycline MIC in experimental systems.
  • Restoring wild-type ramR can reduce tigecycline MIC in a resistant background.
  • T116I weakens the local hydrogen-bond network and is associated with reduced RamR binding to the ramA promoter.
  • Resistant backgrounds show increased ramA/acrB expression and increased AcrAB-TolC/RamA protein abundance.
What remains uncertain
  • The exact size of the resistance effect is not pooled or standardized across studies.
  • Not all entries cleanly isolate one lesion from other co-occurring determinants.
  • The structural model is supportive, not definitive, and lacks reported structure-confidence metrics.
  • Generalizability beyond the captured experimental and paired-isolate contexts is not established.
Client implication

The signal is strong enough to justify continued attention to the RamR/RamA axis, but not strong enough to treat the dossier as a closed case on mechanism, prevalence, or intervention readiness.

Evidence by decision theme

1. Functional effect on tigecycline susceptibility

Evidence strength: Moderate Mechanistic confidence: Moderate Translation/practical confidence: Moderate
Section verdict

Verdict: The dossier supports a real directional effect of RamR-pathway perturbation on tigecycline susceptibility, including experimental evidence that T116I increases MIC and that wild-type ramR complementation reduces MIC.

Why it matters: This is the core commercial question. Without functional phenotype impact, the rest of the mechanistic story has limited buyer value.

Main caution: The dossier does not provide a pooled, buyer-ready estimate of effect size because units, comparators, and reporting granularity differ across entries.

Evidence retained
  • Introducing T116I into a clean background increased tigecycline MIC.
  • A 1 bp insertion in the RamR-binding site upstream of ramA was reported as responsible for tigecycline resistance in an isogenic mutant system.
  • Restoring wild-type ramR in a T116I-containing resistant strain reduced tigecycline MIC.
  • The source dossier interprets these manipulations as consistent with RamR/RamA-axis dysregulation contributing to resistance.

2. Mechanistic plausibility of RamR T116I

Evidence strength: Moderate Mechanistic confidence: Moderate Translation/practical confidence: Low
Section verdict

Verdict: T116I is not just a genomic observation. The dossier provides a plausible mechanistic chain from local structural perturbation to weaker promoter binding, which makes the lesion biologically credible rather than merely associative.

Why it matters: Buyers pay for explanatory value. A lesion that comes with a mechanistic rationale is more valuable than a lesion that only co-occurs with phenotype.

Main caution: The structural component is partly predictive rather than fully experimentally resolved, and confidence metrics for the model are not reported.

Evidence retained
  • AlphaFold3 prediction indicated that T116I reduced the local hydrogen-bond network around residue 116 from six bonds to three bonds without major global structural change.
  • The source interpreted this as likely reducing dimerization-domain stability and altering DNA-binding activity.
  • EMSA showed reduced RamR binding affinity for the ramA promoter in the T116I mutant relative to wild-type RamR.
  • Together these observations were described as consistent with impaired promoter binding caused by local structural perturbation.

3. Pathway-level evidence for efflux dysregulation

Evidence strength: Moderate Mechanistic confidence: Moderate Translation/practical confidence: Moderate
Section verdict

Verdict: Efflux dysregulation is a consistent pathway-level feature of the resistant state in the captured evidence and materially strengthens the RamR/RamA resistance narrative.

Why it matters: This is the bridge from lesion-specific evidence to a broader resistance mechanism that buyers can act on conceptually.

Main caution: Some entries combine RamR, OqxR, and other determinants, so this section supports pathway relevance more strongly than lesion exclusivity.

Evidence retained
  • RamR-linked or OqxR-linked resistant backgrounds were reported to show efflux-pump dysregulation.
  • Resistant strains showed increased ramA and acrB expression.
  • AcrAB-TolC and RamA proteins were increased in resistant isolate comparisons.
  • In a 31-strain survey, tigecycline resistance was mainly caused by ramR or oqxR mutations with efflux-pump overexpression.
  • RamR upregulation at the protein level was interpreted as consistent with altered regulatory function rather than simple loss of expression.

  • One structural component is in silico without reported confidence metrics.
  • One genomic comparison references additional data not shown and supplementary context not fully available.
