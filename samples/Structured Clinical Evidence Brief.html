Design & setting
Study design: Target trial emulation study (observational)
Setting: 67 hospitals in the Michigan Hospital Medicine Safety Consortium
Population & empiric therapy
Population: Adults ≥18 years hospitalized with community-onset sepsis (June 2020–September 2024)
Initial therapy: Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotic (BSA) therapy without evidence of multidrug-resistant organism (MDRO) infection
Comparator structure

Target contrast Continued BSA therapy on day 4 versus BSA de-escalation (no BSA on day 4), with anti-MRSA and anti-PSA de-escalation analyzed as separate target trials.

Primary outcome & sample size
Primary outcome: 90-day all-cause mortality
Total n (community-onset sepsis): 36,924 patients
Anti-MRSA target trial: n = 6,926
Anti-PSA target trial: n = 11,149

Background

Early empiric broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy targeting multidrug-resistant organisms has been associated with lower mortality in higher-risk patients, but prolonged broad-spectrum use is linked to antibiotic-associated adverse events and population-level antimicrobial resistance. Guidelines recommend reassessing empiric antimicrobials within 48–72 hours and de-escalating when MDROs are not identified on initial testing.

Proposed (author interpretation / hypothesis)

Interpretive signals reported by the study authors; not presented as established treatment effects.

  • The mortality signal observed with anti-MRSA de-escalation among clinically stable patients may reflect reverse causation rather than a true treatment effect, because improving clinical trajectory may increase the likelihood of de-escalation. Evidence: Discussion. Quote: "improvements in short-term mortality with de-escalation may be due to reverse causation".

Evidence Base

This brief is grounded in two target trial emulations derived from the HMS-Sepsis registry, comparing day-4 antibiotic de-escalation versus continued broad-spectrum therapy after empiric anti-MRSA or anti-PSA exposure in adults with community-onset sepsis and no target MDRO positivity on encounter days 1–2.

Analytic framework
  • Inverse probability of treatment weighting was used to balance de-escalated and continued-therapy groups on likelihood of de-escalation.
  • Hierarchical logistic and Poisson regression models were applied for outcome analysis.
  • The practical contrast focused on day-4 de-escalation versus day-4 continuation of empiric anti-MRSA or anti-PSA coverage in patients without early target-MDRO positivity.

Proposed (author interpretation / hypothesis)

Gut microbiome and antianaerobic coverage
Mechanistic hypothesis

Anti-PSA coverage may include antianaerobic activity that is deleterious to the gut microbiome and may increase downstream risk of infection and mortality.

Evidence: Discussion. Quote: "Anti-PSA coverage often includes antianaerobic coverage that is deleterious to the gut microbiome and which may increase downstream risk of infection and mortality".
Microbiome preservation and readmission
Mechanistic hypothesis

De-escalating anti-PSA therapy may avoid deleterious broad-spectrum effects on the gut microbiome and thereby reduce downstream risks that contribute to hospital readmission.

Evidence: Discussion. Quote: "One hypothesis for the potential protective effect of de-escalating anti-PSA on hospital readmission is to avoid the deleterious effects of BSA on the gut microbiome and associated downstream risks that may contribute to readmission".

Author-Stated Implications

Proposed implications (author-stated)

Implications are presented as the study authors' interpretations and are not reframed as clinical recommendations.

  • The results suggest that de-escalation of broad-spectrum antibiotics in patients with community-onset sepsis and no positive MDRO test results is associated with similar safety outcomes and may reduce antibiotic exposure and length of hospitalization. Evidence: Key Points / Meaning. Quote: "These results suggest that de-escalation of broad-spectrum antibiotics among patients with community-onset sepsis and no positive MDRO test results is associated with similar safety outcomes and may reduce antibiotic exposure and length of hospitalization".
  • The authors interpret their data as further evidence suggestive that antibiotic de-escalation is safe and appropriate in this setting, supporting existing guideline recommendations. Evidence: Discussion. Quote: "our data lend further evidence suggestive that antibiotic de-escalation is both safe and appropriate in patients hospitalized with community-onset sepsis, bolstering guideline recommendations".
  • Additional prospective randomized studies are needed to further understand the effects of de-escalation for specific classes of antibiotics. Evidence: Discussion. Quote: "Additional prospective randomized studies are needed to further understand the effects of de-escalation of specific classes of antibiotics".

Limitations

Reported limitations
  • Observational study design; residual confounding cannot be excluded. Evidence: Limitations. Quote: "the study is observational and so cannot exclude the possibility of confounding".
  • Calculated E-values were small, so residual confounding could negate observed associations. Evidence: Limitations. Quote: "The calculated E-values are small, so it remains possible that residual confounding could negate the observed results".
  • Some postdischarge mortality outcomes may not have been fully captured. Evidence: Limitations. Quote: "some outcomes may not have been ascertained".
  • Readmission and Clostridioides difficile infection may not have been fully captured and were treated as exploratory outcomes. Evidence: Limitations. Quote: "readmission and C difficile infection may not be fully captured, so we consider these as exploratory outcomes".
  • Repeated hospitalizations within the same individual could not be accounted for because no unique identifier was available. Evidence: Limitations. Quote: "we do not have a unique identifier to identify and account for potential repeated hospitalizations within individuals".

Glossary

Compact terminology support for key methodological and clinical concepts referenced in this brief.

Key terms
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotic (BSA): Empiric antibiotic therapy covering multidrug-resistant organisms. Evidence: Introduction. Quote: "Early empiric broad-spectrum antibiotic (BSA) therapy covering multidrug-resistant organisms (MDROs)".
  • Multidrug-resistant organism (MDRO): Resistant organism category used to define broad-spectrum coverage targets and exclusion criteria. Evidence: Introduction / Methods. Quote: "multidrug-resistant organism (MDRO)".
  • Target trial emulation: Framework for causal inference from observational data, applied here to evaluate de-escalation. Evidence: Methods / Study Cohort and Exposures. Quote: "This study was performed as 2 target trial emulations of BSA de-escalation".
  • Inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW): Method used to balance de-escalated and continued BSA groups on likelihood of de-escalation. Evidence: Methods / Statistical Analysis. Quote: "we used inverse probability of treatment weighting to balance the de-escalated and continued BSA therapy groups".
  • E-value: Estimate of the strength of association a hypothetical unmeasured confounder would need to have to negate the measured association. Evidence: Methods / Statistical Analysis. Quote: "calculated E-values to estimate the strength of association of a hypothetical unmeasured confounder that would be needed to negate the measured associations".
