Bottom line

Ahn et al. (2025) is the strongest inclusion candidate on current evidence because the reported support is the most triangulated: genetic perturbation logic, orthogonal assays, and in vivo validation all point in the same direction.

Decision use

This dossier is suitable for portfolio prioritisation, not for definitive biological adjudication. It compares the strength of the existing appraisals; it does not replace a fresh extraction from the original papers.

Scope of appraisal
Comparative appraisal of prior Quality Check outputs only. No new extraction from the original papers was performed. No meta-analysis was performed. Confidence therefore applies primarily to comparative appraisal quality and decision use, not to every underlying experimental detail.

Executive Recommendation

Recommended order for inclusion
1) Ahn et al. (2025) → core anchor paper. 2) Dev et al. (2026) → strong mechanistic companion with narrower generalisability and a notable confound. 3) Weng et al. (2026) → keep as conditional/supporting inclusion rather than a lead exemplar because external validity is the weakest.

The ranking is driven by comparative decision usefulness and methodological reliability, not by novelty alone or biological importance alone.

Method used for ranking
The three papers were compared using fixed criteria drawn from the prior appraisals: controls and experimental design quality; triangulation across independent evidence types; handling of major confounders; visibility of quantitative/statistical support; external validity / generalisability; and decision usefulness for portfolio inclusion. Ranking reflects which paper is most dependable for comparative decision use on current appraisal evidence, not which appears most novel or biologically important in isolation.

Ranked Assessment

1) Ahn et al. (2025) — lead anchor

Why it ranks first
Among the three, this paper combines the broadest triangulation with the cleanest causal logic. The prior appraisal describes comprehensive controls, reported replicates, clear quantitative outputs, and a low overall risk of bias.
What looks strong
  • Multiple independent evidence modes point in the same direction, which raises confidence beyond any single assay class.
  • Animal validation makes the evidence more decision-relevant than a purely in vitro mechanism paper.
  • The appraisal explicitly identifies the central claim as causal rather than merely associative.
What stays uncertain
  • The signalling bridge between MCR-1 enzymatic activity and transcriptional upregulation is not fully resolved.
  • The study leans on rough-strain backgrounds, which weakens straightforward extrapolation to smoother clinical contexts.
Decision read
Best choice when you want one lead paper whose core claim is both mechanistically persuasive and comparatively well-defended against alternative explanations.

2) Dev et al. (2026) — strong mechanistic companion

Why it ranks second
The study’s internal logic is strong: it tests a specific mechanistic proposition and uses controls designed to distinguish rebinding effects from intrinsic thermodynamic stability. That is highly valuable for mechanistic interpretation.
What looks strong
  • Explicit prediction-testing rather than post hoc storytelling.
  • Appropriate genetic and kinetic controls support the main within-system claim.
  • The appraisal still categorises the evidence as causal.
What stays uncertain
  • Deletion-strain membrane permeability is a live confound that could influence interpretation.
  • The model uses a single reporter/drug-target framework, which narrows generalisability to clinical antibiotics.
  • Some key kinetic comparisons were not fully statistically annotated in the reported appraisal.
Decision read
High-value as a mechanistic insight paper, but less suitable than Ahn et al. as the single best exemplar if you need broader translational confidence.

3) Weng et al. (2026) — include only with explicit caveat

Why it ranks third
This paper remains interesting and usable, but the link between the model and the claimed clinical reality is the weakest in the set. The strongest concern is not novelty; it is external validity.
What looks strong
  • The within-model story is coherent and quantitatively reported.
  • Controls and replicates were reported, and the appraisal still supports inclusion.
  • The paper may still be useful for hypothesis generation around plasmid heterogeneity.
What stays uncertain
  • The high-copy vector model in E. coli DH5α may distort the dynamics being invoked to support broader clinical claims.
  • Model fitting and evaluation on the same dataset reduces confidence in the mathematical layer.
  • Direct clinical extrapolation should be treated as conditional rather than established.
Decision read
Worth keeping in the portfolio, but only as a supporting or speculative piece. It should not carry the main weight of a methodological argument.

Comparative Weighting of Evidence

Evidence I would trust most
The strongest evidence in this set is the convergent causal chain in Ahn et al., because the appraisal describes multiple perturbational and phenotypic layers agreeing with each other, including in vivo support. The next most useful is the clean mechanistic separation in Dev et al., which appears internally well reasoned even if not maximally generalisable.
Evidence I would discount first
The weakest evidence for broad decision use is any claim in Weng et al. that assumes the model directly mirrors native clinical plasmid behaviour. I would also discount any overextension of Dev et al. from its reporter system to broad antibiotic behaviour without further corroboration.

Limitations That Matter for the Reader

  • This dossier inherits the strengths and blind spots of the earlier Quality Check outputs.
  • No contradictions were identified across the three papers, but they also do not test the same biological question, so agreement should not be mistaken for direct replication.
  • Randomisation and blinding were generally not reported across the set, which matters more for confidence in experimental rigor than the narrative summaries alone may suggest.
  • All three papers remain includable, but they should not be treated as equally decision-reliable.

Final Recommendation

Comparative conclusion
Ahn et al. (2025) is the best lead anchor because the prior appraisal supports the most robust comparative combination of control quality, causal triangulation, quantitative visibility, and in vivo relevance. Dev et al. (2026) is the strongest supporting mechanistic paper: its core within-system claim appears well reasoned and decision-useful, but confidence is moderated by a live membrane-permeability confound and less complete statistical visibility in some central comparisons. Weng et al. (2026) should remain conditional because the main limitation is external validity rather than internal coherence; the within-model result may be useful, but broader clinical interpretation should stay explicitly bounded. What can be said with confidence is the comparative ordering Ahn > Dev > Weng for portfolio use on current appraisal evidence. What should remain caveated is any stronger claim about absolute biological truth, full translational relevance, or paper-level superiority beyond the limits of the existing Quality Check outputs.
